Joining the Data Cognition Lab

This page provides information for prospective students and postdocs about what we look for in lab members and other frequently asked questions. In the 2025 cycle, we are actively recruiting one postdoc. Undergraduate and masters students should reach out with inquiries about getting involved in research.

Active Projects

These are active projects in the lab where we are seeking to recruit help.

Knowledge Mobilization for Decision-Making in Education

This interdisciplinary collaboration aims to reinvent science communication for evidence-based decision-making in education. Currently, the US Department of Education hosts websites like What Works Clearinghouse to summarize scientific evidence on educational interventions and their outcomes, with the goal that teachers, principles, superintendents, etc. can make more efficacious choices in their classrooms, schools, and disticts. Together with statistics Profs. Beth Tipton and Kaitlyn Fitzgerald, we proposed to redesign the summaries of scientific evidence on these platforms using empirical software testing and user-centered design practices.

This project entails solving problems around visualizing statistical results from scientific research, summarizing and modeling studies that vary in their research designs, helping non-statisticians reason about sources of uncertainty in data, and designing software for to help scientists create actionable decision-aids based on education research. This touches on important and timely topics such as causal inference, fairness, and algorithmic assistance for human judgment and decision-making.

Read more our previous work on this topic:
  • A Knowledge Mobilization Framework: Toward Evidence-Based Statistical Communication Practices in Education Research
  • The Value of Variation: Embracing Heterogeneity in Intervention Research
  • MetaExplorer: Facilitating Reasoning with Epistemic Uncertainty in Meta-analysis
  • The Meta-Analytic Rain Cloud Plot: A New Approach to Visualizing Clearinghouse Data

    • This project is ideal for a postdoc with a strong interest in statistics and human-centered software development. We envision recruiting someone from either background to help us co-design tools with education researchers and decision-makers. Join us to create a new standard for communicating about meta-analysis and evidence aggregation!

    Participatory Budgeting in Chicago

    This project is an effort to use visualization research for social good in Chicago. The City currently uses a process called participatory budgeting to include residents in decision-making about how local funding is spent within each Ward. We are interested in using visualization to help facilitate this process, e.g., through more expressive interfaces for residents to express their preferences, or through outreach activities that use visualization to promote transparancy and perspective-taking around collective decisions.

    This project cuts across problems in visualization, digital civics, voting methods, and preference elicitation. It synthesizes ideas and methods from HCI, economics, political science, and urban planning. Read about our initial foray into this topic in our recent paper: What Can Interactive Visualization do for Participatory Budgeting in Chicago?

    Frequently Asked Questions

    The following are questions that we receive frequently from prospective students. Our answers to these questions will help you prepare a successful application to research programs at UChicago and will help you decide if the Data Cognition Lab is a good fit.

    What should I include in a written research statement?

    Successful PhD and postdoc applications typically submit three strong letters of recommendation along with an excellently-written statement telling a story about how their research experiences and interests align with one or two potential advisors. Research statements should propose possible research project(s) the applicant would be interested in working on. These are meant to demonstrate how you think about research and how your interests align concretely with the potential advisor(s) you identify.

    What is the lab currently working on?

    We are currently focused on creating visualizations and statistical tools to support data exploration and data sharing, appraisal of the intrumental value of datasets, strategic and personalized design of visualizations, model interpretability, and decision-making.

    What academic background do I need to join the lab?

    We invite scholars from a variety of academic backgrounds since the nature of our work is highly interdisciplinary. Backgrounds in the behavioral sciences are highly relevant because we design experiments and build cognitive models of human behavior. Backgrounds in computer science and software engineering are highly relevant because we build toolkits, prototypes, and systems to make ideas usable, test hypotheses, and demonstrate proof-of-concept. Backgrounds in statistics and research methodology are highly relevant becasue many of our projects involve aspects of modeling, uncertainty, and epistemology in creating new ways to work with data. Backgrounds in human-computer interaction and user experience research are highly relevant because we tend to address questions about people's technology use, and we need to run interview-based user studies.

    What does Alex look for in prospective PhD students?

    The two primary things that I look for in prospective advisees are research fit and intrinsic motivation. Research fit involves your taste in projects, the questions you ask, and way you think about research more broadly. Some of this has to do with familiarity with and interest in my research area; we need to be able to agree on projects to pursue. Some of it has to do with skills; we need to be able to execute the work we might want to do. Mostly this has to do with creative energy; we need to be excited about meeting to talk about research for 5+ years, and those conversations need to generate new ideas.

    Intrinsic motivation (and time) is most of what it takes to do a PhD. Research can be difficult. There will be unexpected obstacles and periods of doubt when you need grit and a certain level of commitment to push through. I look for intrinsic motivation because I want students taking this journey with the lab to succeed.

    What does Alex look for in prospective postdocs?

    In prospective postdocs, I seek partners who will lead a mix of independent and supervised research in the lab on topics of mutual interest. Postdocs in the DCL should pursue research that advances both my research agenda and their own simultaniously. This could look a lot of different ways and could entail publishing in a variety of venues or disciplines. However, prospective postdocs should know that I am most equipped to help you publish top-tier research in visualization and human-computer interaction, and have a professional imperative to submit work to computer science conferences, which will shape your experience working with me. Postdocs should have a strong interest in mentoring students and generally learning the skills involved in managing a research group.

    How can undergraduate and master's students get involved in the lab?

    We usually recruit people when there is a strong match in research interests and/or when an ongoing project in the lab needs more people working on it. We will post descriptions of active projects we are recruiting for above. Typically, undergraduate or masters students will be paired with a PhD student or postdoc to help supervise their work. In cases where an undergraduate or masters student has demonstrated exceptional ability and committment to research, the lab is open to hosting student-led research projects.

    Where do I apply for positions in the lab?

    We are currently recruiting PhD students through UChicago's PhD programs in Data Science and Computer Science.

    Current opportunities for postdoc positions are through the Data Science Institute's postdoc scholars program.

    We recruit undergraduate and masters students on a per project basis. The best ways to find out about these positions are to email Alex and to keep an eye on this page.

    Contact

    Prospective mentees who are interested in joining the Data Cognition Lab may contact Alex Kale via email (kalea@uchicago.edu). Please be aware that decisions about admission to degree programs and other competative positions in the lab will follow a formal application process.

    If you do reach out to Alex to express an interest in a position, please include the the following information in your message: (1) Your CV and a link to a portfolio of relevant projects; (2) Why do you want to join the DCL? (3) Why are you interested in whatever position you are applying for (if relevant), e.g., why do you want to do a PhD or postdoc?
